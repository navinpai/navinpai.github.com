What?
"The Google Summer of Code, often abbreviated to GSoC, is an international annual program,
first held from May to August 2005, in which Google awards stipends (of US$5,500, as of 2016 )
to all students who successfully complete a requested free and open-source software coding
project during the summer."
Long story short, it's a program which brings together students and organizations in the
open source space, to work together over 3 months to create something of value!

Organizations participating in this involve a lot of the big names we use on a regular basis
(PSF, Mozilla, Gnome, Debian, Django, FFMpeg, Github, Moodle, NMap, OpenCV, Ruby,
ScalaLang, Tor Project, Wikimedia,  X).
Why?
  • - GSoC is an amazing platform to get started with 
  • contributing to open source projects

   - Actually contribute to tools you use, and have your code 
    used by potentially millions across the world!

  • - It is a well defined program so you know what you 
  • are getting into

  • - Recognition, and a killer line on your resume

  • - Quite a bit of $$$ + Google swag!

How?
The list of GSoC companies remains fairly static year over year, with a few additions and
deletions. Go through the list of GSoC orgs from 2015/16 to get a fair idea of which ones 
you can apply to.

Once you identify orgs, look for a) a mailing list and b) a GSoC ideas page. Many orgs have 
a publicly accessible mailing list you can sign up on, and a GSoC ideas page which list out
potential GSoC ideas.






Introduce yourself on the mailing lists and get started with discussing the why's, what's and 
how's of different projects in the org.
Eg: 
https://community.kde.org/GSoC/2016/Ideas
https://honeynet.org/gsoc/ideas
http://elinux.org/BeagleBoard/GSoC/Ideas
https://wiki.gnome.org/Outreach/SummerOfCode/2016/Ideas
How?
How?
Identify potential mentors from the mailing list and the project commits. This is a big deal
because this will be your guide over 3 months and your primary communication. Ask for
project ideas and improvements, guidelines and pitfalls, and work together with the org to
decide on what would be a good proposal to make.

Your only goal is to stand out. Even fixing minor issues, or adding documentation to known
processes, or writing tutorials goes a long way. It's all about letting people know that you
want to contribute.

Even when you make a proposal, make sure you let the org know about your proposal and
work with your potential mentor to clean up the proposal.
How?
Selected candidates have a specified Community bonding period, which is the time given for
you to actually become part of the org. Understand their tooling, understand how they
communicate, how to look at the bigger picture, the coding standards to follow (No more
hackathon type code).

A big part of this is communication, so make sure you can communicate. Most orgs are VERY
VERY understanding about timelines, but not if you spring it out of the blue. You're in GSoC
to learn, and they're here to help you! Make the most of the opportunity

Tips
Is the experience worth it? Duh.

How much time would I be working on GSoC? During the actual 3 months, about 20-30
hours per week is the recommended amount of time.

Would I recommend it to 4th Sem M.Tech Students? Not really unless you want to give up on
your internship / social life

Should I apply even if I'm only a decent coder? Yes, you should. Coding is one part of GSoC, 
but there are people who have used GSoC as an opportunity to actually learn proper coding

Can I submit multiple proposals? Yes, upto 5. But it's recommended to keep it limited.
